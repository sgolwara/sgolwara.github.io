---
title: At the Crossroads
date: 2026-03-07
---

God. An all-knowing, all-seeing, omnipotent entity — the creator of the universe and of us. It is a concept I have seen in many different lights across different periods of my life. My early childhood was spent listening to stories about the gods of the Indian Pantheon. Ganesha's wisdom, Hanuman's mischief, Shiva's wrath, and Indra's ego were characters of great fascination to me. A few years later, I began to question whether these characters could actually exist. Could an elephant-headed man survive from a biological standpoint? Was it truly possible for a living being to wield lightning and thunder as weapons? Could divine powers exist within the very laws that divinity itself supposedly created? I wasn't so sure anymore. With a growing knowledge of science — gathered from school and the boundless corners of the internet — I began to question the legitimacy of this power we call God. By around the age of eleven, guided by science and logic, I reached the point where I could no longer honestly say I believed. Now, only seven years later, my opinion has grown far more nuanced. Not entirely believing, yet not entirely disbelieving either. I stand back at the crossroads — sometimes wondering whether I should make a choice at all.

**Turn Left.** "Path of God." "Path of the Light." "Path of Ignorance." It goes by many names, but they all describe the same thing: belief in a higher power. God tends to give people hope. Hope, though, is a fragile thing — often shattered by reality. When that hope breaks, the God who once provided it quickly transforms from a savior into a scapegoat. This power becomes the object of blame for millions. *"Why did you do this to us? Did we deserve such a life?"* Such questions arise even in the minds of believers. It was this mindset that began to erode my own faith. Was God simply something to blame? Was there ever a being who decided that the world needed someone to curse — someone to carry the weight of all its suffering? We like to say that God is responsible for all the good in the world too, but in the grand scheme of things, it can be hard to see that good. So what if God isn't meant to be worshipped, but scorned? What if that, too, is a path to God? With these questions turning in my mind, I began to pull away from the omnipotent being. I began to see myself as my own God — capable of making my own decisions and living with their consequences. I turned to atheism. My newfound beliefs did shock my parents. I still remember telling them I didn't feel at peace in temples, which was honest — and I suspected most people didn't either. I came to believe that many people's faith was grounded less in genuine feeling and more in a herd mentality. I was probably not so different. I was simply more aware that something wasn't sitting right. And so I reached the end of that fork, turned back, and at the crossroads — without hesitation — turned right.

**Turn Right.** The Path of Science. Of Rationality. People think it means hating or rejecting God. It doesn't. It means becoming your own God. When your understanding of that radiant, transcendent force of beauty and hope is dismantled, you crave something that restores a sense of balance. I found my balance in the laws of science and my footing in rational thought. When good and bad are no longer defined by sins and virtues handed down by religious structures, you are forced to build your own definitions. It was during this time that I arrived at the definition of good that I think most people, in practice, already use: continuity. The continuation of what matters most to you. We all carry a quiet internal ranking of what we value. When we need to decide the right thing to do, we consult that ranking and choose to protect what sits highest on it. Armed with my own mind and the principles of science, I felt like my own person. My individuality was an unwavering flame. I was the tallest tower in my small world. And then — ego. The river that carves through the greatest mountains. My tower could not stand forever. My small world kept expanding as I grew up. People and ideas I had never considered began to reshape my thinking. The self-reliance that had built me up began, eventually, to pull me apart. And so I walked the only path I knew: back to the crossroads.

**Back at the crossroads.** I stand here today, still uncertain whether God is real. I now try to hold both directions in mind at once. The things that once seemed so clear and settled now reveal themselves to be part of something far more uncertain — a question that has knocked at my door again and again. And I've arrived at a conclusion: what if God simply doesn't matter? Not in a nihilistic sense — more in a practical one. God doesn't really interfere. Whether God exists or not shouldn't change how I live my life. I no longer reject God. I still believe, in some sense, that I am my own God — but not out of ego or rebellion anymore. It's simply this: if God doesn't exist, then I am writing my own future. If God does exist, then my actions are still laying the path toward whatever I may receive later. Either way, the purpose remains the same. Take action. Take action toward what you believe is right. After that — whether through causality or through God — you will receive what you're meant to receive. Honestly, I've stopped feeling the need to fixate on outcomes. As long as I do what I believe is right, I will get what I deserve. If I don't deserve something, it won't come to me. My life, with or without God, will be an accumulation of results that stem from my own choices. And if I hold onto that — if there is a God — maybe I will attain moksha. Or heaven. I'm not entirely sure, but that's a conversation for another time.
